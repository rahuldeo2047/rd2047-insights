§ 1

Why NMR is the Safe Architecture

Nuclear Magnetic Resonance uses the quantum spin of atomic nuclei — not electron spins in a diamond defect — as the qubit. The physics is inherently low-energy and safe.

In an NMR quantum computer, hydrogen nuclei (protons) in a water or chemical sample are placed in a magnetic field. This field aligns their nuclear spins — each proton becomes a qubit. A brief radio-frequency pulse at the Larmor precession frequency rotates these spins, implementing quantum gates. A pickup coil detects the tiny voltage induced as the spins precess back.

Key insight: NMR transitions have energies in the microelectronvolt range — billions of times lower than the electron-volt energies involved in NV-center laser excitation. This is why NMR is intrinsically safe: there simply isn't enough energy involved to harm biological tissue or require shielding.

NMR quantum computing was pioneered in 1997–2000 at MIT, Oxford, and IBM, and remains the most accessible hardware platform for demonstrating real quantum gate operations. NMR implementations are user-friendly and remain popular for proof-of-principle demonstrations of quantum information protocols.

§ 4

Honest Trade-offs vs. NV-Center

Eliminating hazards comes with real physics trade-offs. These should be understood before committing to the NMR architecture.

⚖ Scalability Ceiling (~7 qubits)
NMR is arguably the best technology for implementing simple quantum computing experiments and the worst technology for building large-scale quantum computers — after implementations of Shor's algorithm in a 7-spin system, the field started to reach its natural limits. NV-center approaches in principle scale further. For proof-of-concept and education, NMR is excellent; for frontier research, it is not.
⚖ Ensemble Readout (Not Single-Shot)
NMR measures the average magnetization of ~10¹⁸ molecules, not a single qubit. This is a fundamental limitation: single-qubit projective measurement is not achievable with liquid NMR. The NV-center APD can (in principle) detect a single spin. For many quantum algorithms, ensemble averaging is sufficient — but not for all.
⚖ No True Ground-State Initialization
Liquid NMR at room temperature uses "pseudo-pure states" — approximate initializations achieved through clever pulse sequences rather than true cooling to the ground state. This reduces effective qubit fidelity compared to an NV-center at room temperature which can be initialized to |0⟩ with ~95% fidelity via the laser.
✓ What NMR Does Better
Despite the trade-offs, NMR retains significant advantages for the DIY and educational context:
  • Coherence times: T₂ up to seconds in some molecules — far exceeding most room-temperature NV-center builds
  • Gate fidelity: Well-characterized, high-fidelity gates using GRAPE optimal control pulses
  • Software toolchain: Mature open-source stack (SpinQKit, Qiskit NMR backend, SIMPSON simulator)
  • No environment engineering: Works on a desk, in a classroom, with no blackout box, no isolation table
  • Documented algorithms: Shor's, Grover's, Deutsch-Jozsa, quantum teleportation all demonstrated in literature
  • Community: 200+ university deployments via SpinQ alone; large academic support base

Recommended Build Sequence

Start with Tier 1. Validate the physics at each step before investing in more hardware.

1
Simulate NMR gates in SIMPSON or QuTiP
SIMPSON (open-source) simulates NMR pulse sequences on spin systems. QuTiP (Python) provides general open quantum systems simulation. Validate your Rabi nutation, Hahn echo, and CNOT gate designs in software before soldering a single component.
Week 1 · Free
2
Build Tier 1 — Earth-field NMR with Raspberry Pi Pico
Replicate the Hackaday open-source Earth-field NMR design. Observe a free induction decay (FID) peak in the FFT at 2kHz — this is your first real quantum spin signal. The entire build uses USB power and copper wire. Zero safety concerns.
Week 2–4 · ~$100
3
Demonstrate Hahn Echo — measure T₂
Apply π/2 — wait τ — π — wait τ — detect. The echo peak at time 2τ proves coherent spin manipulation. The amplitude decay as you increase τ measures T₂ — your qubit's coherence time. This is real quantum physics with a coil of wire.
Week 4–6
4
Upgrade to Tier 2 — add permanent magnet for multi-qubit
Add a 0.3T Halbach magnet array and switch the sample to ¹³C-labeled chloroform (CHCl₃). Now you have two addressable nuclear spins at different frequencies. Implement a CNOT gate via J-coupling free evolution. This is a genuine 2-qubit quantum processor.
Month 2–3 · ~$400 total
5
Run Deutsch-Jozsa algorithm
The Deutsch-Jozsa algorithm determines whether a function is constant or balanced using a single query — exponentially faster than any classical algorithm. It requires 1 qubit + 1 ancilla and can be fully implemented on a 2-qubit NMR system. This is your first provably quantum speedup demonstration.
Month 3–4
6
Optionally: access cloud quantum systems for scale
For algorithms requiring more than 3–5 qubits, switch to IBM Quantum Experience or AWS Braket. Your NMR knowledge transfers directly — quantum gate operations, pulse sequences, Bloch sphere intuition all apply. You've learned on real hardware; now you can scale on cloud hardware safely.
