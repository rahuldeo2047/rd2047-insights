Act 0  ·  The Problem

Two unsolved problems sit under every model

Memory. A frontier model is stateless. Every conversation begins from zero. A bigger context window is not memory — it is a longer notepad that costs O(N²) to read and that the model attends to sub-linearly past 128K. Retrieval bolts a database on the side, but the model ignores what it retrieves a meaningful fraction of the time.


Hallucination. Factual error rates at the frontier still run 10–20%. This is not a corner case — it is the central reason these systems cannot be trusted in domains where being wrong has a cost.

The reframe
Every existing approach treats memory as storage. The brain treats memory as compression into structure — and runs a verification step before it trusts a recalled fact. INFMEM-12B is built around that distinction.
Act 1  ·  The Moat

Why nobody is building this

The problem is known. The components exist in published research. So why is the integrated system not already shipping from a frontier lab? Because the incentives point the other way. The reluctance of everyone else is the moat.

01
It crosses too many fields
Neuroscience + ML architecture + knowledge engineering + systems + alignment. Funders back one problem at a time.
02
It can't be one paper
Each component is publishable alone. The integration is an engineering thesis — and academia rewards novelty per component, not assembly.
03
Labs bet on scale
More data, more parameters. The wager is that scale dissolves memory and hallucination on its own. So far it hasn't.
04
The training is slow
Test-time training meta-learning runs several times slower than standard pretraining at short context. Most teams see the cost and stop.
05
"I don't know" doesn't ship
A model that abstains looks bad on engagement metrics. Product managers remove the abstainer. A solo builder has no PM.
06
The unsexy work
Knowledge-graph curation and integration plumbing have no leaderboard and no tweet. So they get skipped — and the gap stays open.
The thesis in one line
The gap between research and product is exactly the invisible integration work nobody wants to do. Hard to replicate, cross-domain, not stealable by a single paper. That is what makes it defensible.
Act 2  ·  The Approach

Architecture borrowed from a brain

Instead of inventing structure, INFMEM-12B maps known brain physiology onto an AI pipeline. Each region's job becomes a layer's job. The high-level correspondence is below. The exact mechanism inside each layer — how memory is compressed, how the verifiers reach consensus, how the expert pool is merged — is the part that took the work, and it stays in the paper.

Working memory (PFC)
A deliberately short attention window — short on purpose, which forces harder long-term compression.
Hippocampus
Context compressed into weights at inference time, so recall cost stays flat no matter how long the session runs.
Cerebellum
A pool of domain experts; the right ones are selected and combined per question.
External databases
A knowledge ground used to cross-check claims that compression alone can't be trusted to hold exactly.
Prefrontal cortex
A panel of independent verifiers that vote before output is released — and abstain when they disagree.
Sleep consolidation
A nightly loop that ingests new information, filters it, and updates the knowledge ground while the machine is idle.
A demerit flipped into a merit
The single most counter-intuitive finding driving this design: a shorter attention window produces better long-term memory, because it forces the model to compress harder rather than lean on a long buffer. What looked like a constraint became the feature. There are several more reversals like this — they are the heart of the paper.

Act 4  ·  Foundation

Standing on real, checkable research

The design is not invented from nothing. Every load-bearing claim traces to published work. These are real papers — verified, linked, and worth reading on their own.

End-to-End Test-Time Training for Long Context arXiv:2512.23675
Compress context into weights via next-token prediction; constant decode latency with scaling comparable to full attention. The memory primitive.
Local Mixtures of Experts: Essentially Free Test-Time Training via Model Merging arXiv:2505.14136 · ETH Zürich
Scale the expert paradigm by an order of magnitude and merge weights at test time with almost no overhead — over 100× faster than naive test-time training. The expert-routing basis.
Short window attention enables long-term memorization arXiv:2509.24552 · Meta FAIR
Larger sliding windows hurt long-context performance; a shorter window forces better use of long-term memory. The counter-intuitive design lever.
Cross-Layer Attention Probing for Fine-Grained Hallucination Detection arXiv:2509.09700
Detect likely hallucinations from a model's own internal activations across the residual stream — no external ground truth required. One of the verifiers.
Mitigating LLM Hallucinations via Autonomous Knowledge-Graph-based Retrofitting arXiv:2311.13314
Verify and retrofit draft responses against a knowledge graph during reasoning. The factual-grounding verifier.
Simple and Scalable Strategies to Continually Pre-train Large Language Models arXiv:2403.08763
Learning-rate re-warming plus a small replay buffer matches full retraining — preventing catastrophic forgetting during continual updates. The nightly-learning anchor.
Hallucination-Free? Assessing the Reliability of Leading AI Legal Research Tools arXiv:2405.20362 · Stanford HAI / RegLab
The preregistered study behind the 17–34% legal-AI hallucination figures cited above. The problem this architecture exists to solve.
